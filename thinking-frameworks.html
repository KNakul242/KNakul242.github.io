The Hidden Mechanics of Thinking

Bayesian, Frequentist, Evidential, Causal — four frameworks in a century-long argument. They were never competing. They were answering different questions.

The Moment Revealing the Concept

You wake up. Your back hurts. Without thinking, you reach a conclusion: probably slept badly.

Hold that moment. Before you stretched, before you reached for your phone, your brain ran a reasoning process so automatic you didn't notice it had structure. It had evidence. It had a prior belief. It formed a conclusion. And somewhere underneath that conclusion, a second question was already forming: what should I do about it?

These are two genuinely different questions. The first is about inference — how do you reason from evidence to belief? The second is about decision — how do you act on that belief? Most of us treat them as one motion. They are not. And the four frameworks that philosophers and statisticians have built to answer them — Bayesian, Frequentist, Evidential, Causal — have been in productive, unresolved conflict for over a century precisely because they answer different questions.

What Does Probability Even Mean?

Before Bayesian versus Frequentist, there is a more primitive question: when you say there is a 30% chance of rain, what does that number refer to?

There are three candidate answers at the foundational level.

Frequency: probability is a property of the world. It is the long-run frequency of an event across infinite repetitions of identical conditions — the number exists independent of any observer. Degree of belief: probability is a property of an agent's mind. It is how confident you are, given what you currently know. Propensity: probability is a property of the situation itself — an objective tendency or disposition built into the setup, neither pure frequency nor pure belief. Karl Popper held this view. A radioactive atom has a propensity to decay; that propensity exists whether or not anyone measures it or repeats the experiment.

The propensity view has a fatal problem: it cannot be measured. A tendency that produces no observable frequency and requires no agent to hold it is doing philosophical work without empirical grounding. Apply Occam's razor and it falls away. What remains are two defensible positions — frequency and belief — which map directly onto Frequentism and Bayesianism.

Frequentism commits to the first. Probability is objective, out there in the world, measurable across repeated trials. Its wound is the reference class problem: who decides which events count as sufficiently identical? For a one-off question — will this specific startup succeed? — frequentism cannot assign a probability at all. There is no reference class of identical startups in identical conditions.

Bayesianism commits to the second. Probability is your degree of confidence, updated as new evidence arrives via Bayes' rule: posterior belief equals prior belief adjusted by how well the evidence fits. Its wound is the prior problem: two people starting with different priors will reach different conclusions from identical evidence, and the framework cannot tell you whose starting point was more legitimate.

The clearest illustration: flip a coin, catch it, cover it with your palm. You can see it. I cannot. What is the probability it landed heads? A Frequentist says there is no probability — the coin has already landed, the outcome is fixed at 100% or 0%, and my ignorance does not change the fact. A Bayesian says 50% — because probability is a statement about my belief state, and my belief state is symmetric. Same coin. Same moment. Two completely different answers, each internally coherent, each following from a different answer to the foundational question.

Bayesianism wins on parsimony. Frequentism requires an infinite hypothetical sequence of identical trials that can never actually exist. Bayesianism needs only an agent with beliefs and a rule for updating them. For most of daily life, you are a Bayesian whether you know it or not.

Does Your Action Change Anything?

The inference question understood, a second and separate question emerges. You have a belief. Now what do you do?

Evidential Decision Theory (EDT) says: choose the action that correlates most strongly with good outcomes. Do what is evidence of things going well.

Causal Decision Theory (CDT) says: choose the action that causes the best outcome. Correlation is not enough. You need a mechanism.

These come apart in a way that is both philosophically sharp and completely practical. Consider the medical Newcomb problem.

In the 1950s, data showed that smokers got cancer at far higher rates. The tobacco industry's counter-argument was structurally sophisticated: perhaps a gene exists that simultaneously causes the desire to smoke and causes cancer. Smoking and cancer are correlated — but if the gene is the common cause of both, smoking does not cause cancer. It is merely a symptom of the same underlying condition.

EDT says Don't smoke — it is powerful evidence you will get cancer. The correlation is real and your action should track it.
CDT says If the gene theory is correct, smoking has no causal effect on cancer. Smoke if you enjoy it. Your action cannot change what the gene has already determined.

Most people's gut says EDT is right here. CDT's answer feels cold. But CDT is formally correct if you fully accept the causal model — and the critical point is that you genuinely cannot rule out the gene theory from observational data alone. The asymmetry in outcome frequency does not tell you the mechanism. This is not a philosophical technicality. It is the deepest reason randomised controlled trials exist: only an intervention — randomly assigning people to smoke or not — can break the symmetry and isolate cause from correlation.

No amount of observational data, however massive, answers an interventional question. The leap from association to causation requires a different kind of assumption entirely.

The Grid

The four frameworks are not competing answers to one question. They are answers to two different questions, and they form a grid. The horizontal axis is inference — how you reason from evidence. The vertical axis is decision — how you act on it.

Bayesian + Evidential How most daily reasoning works. Dark clouds form — you update your belief that rain is likely, and take an umbrella. Prior beliefs updated by observation, action guided by correlation. No experiment, no mechanism modelled.
Frequentist + Evidential Traditional correlational statistics. In the 1950s, researchers counted smokers and cancer rates across thousands of patients, found a strong pattern, and advised against smoking — without yet knowing the mechanism. Powerful for describing patterns; silent on what causes them.
Bayesian + Causal A doctor holds a prior belief that a drug works based on past trials, then updates it with a new patient's test results — and reasons about the mechanism to choose the right dosage. Belief updated by evidence, action governed by cause.
Frequentist + Causal The randomised controlled trial. Researchers randomly assign patients to drug or placebo, measure outcomes across both groups, and isolate the drug's causal effect from all other variables. No prior beliefs — only intervention and measurement.

Notice what this grid makes visible: you can be Bayesian without being Evidential. You can be Causal without running experiments. The frameworks are orthogonal. Mix them up and the debate becomes noise — each side arguing past the other because they are not answering the same question.

Pearl's Ladder: Why More Data Is Never Enough

Judea Pearl, who built the mathematics of causal reasoning, observed that these frameworks are not merely different preferences — they occupy different levels of a hierarchy, each strictly more powerful than the one below.

Layer One — Association

What correlates with what? It answers: given that I observe X, what should I expect? In the 1950s, researchers observed that smokers got cancer at far higher rates than non-smokers. That is a layer-one finding — a pattern in data. It does not tell you whether smoking caused the cancer, or whether something else caused both. The pattern is real. The explanation is not yet settled.

Layer Two — Intervention

What happens if I make X occur? This layer asks about the effect of a deliberate action, not a passive observation. To answer whether smoking causes cancer, you would need to randomly assign people to smoke or not — and measure what follows. The tobacco companies' genetic argument was a layer-two claim: even if the correlation is real, they argued, the mechanism runs through a gene, not through the cigarette. You cannot refute that with more observational data. You need an experiment.

Layer Three — Counterfactual

What would have happened if X had not occurred? A court asking whether a specific patient's lung cancer was caused by decades of smoking is not asking about population correlations or trial averages — it is asking about one person, one history, one outcome that cannot be re-run. This layer requires the deepest causal model: one that can reason about worlds that did not happen.

Pearl's sharpest insight: you cannot get from layer one to layer two by accumulating more data. No observational dataset, however massive, answers an interventional question. The tobacco companies' genetic argument was formally valid precisely because it was a layer-two claim being evaluated with layer-one data. The gene theory could not be ruled out by correlation alone. You needed an experiment.

This is not a limitation of computing power or sample size. It is structural. The information required to answer a causal question is simply not present in observational data. You need a different kind of input — an intervention — not more of the same input.

How the Three Mental Models Navigate the Grid

Given four frameworks and three layers of a hierarchy, the practical question is: how do you know which to use? Three mental models triangulate to a single method.

First principles thinking Ask what layer of Pearl's hierarchy your problem actually lives at. Not what layer your existing data answers — what layer the question requires. A startup founder asking "will this product succeed" is asking a layer-one question and should know that. A doctor choosing a treatment is asking a layer-two question and should not settle for layer-one evidence. The framework follows from the question. Start with the question.
Second-order thinking Every framework's failure mode is a violated assumption. Frequentism fails when there is no stable reference class. Bayesianism misleads when your prior is badly wrong and your data is too thin to correct it. EDT leads you astray when correlation is driven by a common cause you have not modelled. CDT fails when you have the wrong causal graph. The second-order question is always: which assumption does my situation most severely stress?
Occam's razor Do not invoke a deeper layer of the hierarchy than your problem requires. If your question lives at layer one, Bayesian updating is sufficient and elegant. Reaching for a full causal model when you need only update a belief is not rigour — it is unnecessary complexity. The razor cuts both ways: don't oversimplify a causal question into a correlational one, but don't over-engineer an observational question into a controlled experiment.

The three principles are not a checklist. They are a lens. First principles identifies the layer. Second-order thinking reveals the hidden cost of each framework at that layer. Occam's razor selects the simplest framework whose assumptions your problem does not violate.

Back to the Sore Back

You woke up. Your back hurt. You thought: probably slept badly.

That first move was Bayesian inference at layer one — a prior belief about sleep and back pain, updated by the evidence of the pain itself. A layer-one question: given what I observe, what should I expect?

Then a second question formed. If you thought "bad sleep correlates with back pain, so I'll try sleeping differently" — that was Evidential, still at layer one. You acted on the association. If you thought "bad sleep causes muscle tension which causes pain; I need to fix the sleep mechanism" — that was Causal, and it moved you to layer two. You were no longer asking what correlates with what. You were asking what, if changed, would produce a different outcome.

Both moves in the same morning. One question about evidence. One question about action. The frameworks were doing different jobs without announcing themselves.

They were never in competition. They were answering different questions. The confusion was in thinking there was only one question to ask.

Your back hurts. You reach for painkiller, stretching, or a new mattress. Which framework is each action using — and which layer of Pearl's hierarchy does each answer live at?
