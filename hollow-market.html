The Hollow Market

AI will not destroy the economy through malice or catastrophe. It will do something quieter and more complete — it will remove the tension that makes the economy worth having.

The Ground Rules

Most arguments about AI begin with the technology. This one begins earlier — with what a market actually is, and why that question matters more than any forecast about jobs or growth or displacement.

Three foundations sit underneath everything that follows. They are not claimed as original. They are claimed as structural — meaning the argument does not work without them, and if you find yourself resisting the conclusions, the disagreement is probably here, not further along.

First: a market is not a mechanism. It is a mirror. It reflects, at every moment, the aggregate of human desire — what people want, what they lack, what they are willing to sacrifice to close that gap. The price signal is not just a coordination tool. It is a direction signal. It tells the system not only what things cost but what things matter. That signal has one source: the unsatisfied human observer. Not the satisfied one — a satisfied observer generates no signal. The engine of the market is specifically human dissatisfaction, the drive toward improvement that has no natural ceiling because humans, uniquely, can always imagine better than what they have.

Consider a perfect product. A product so complete, so frictionless, so exactly what users need, that no one reports a problem, no one requests a feature, no one complains. That product's development stops. Not because the company chose to stop. Because the signal that tells it where to go next has gone silent. The market is this dynamic, writ civilizationally large. It moves because humans are never fully satisfied. Remove that incompleteness and you do not improve the market. You switch it off.

Second: the market is downstream of society, not the other way around. Society came first — the tribe, the community, the shared project of making collective life better. The market was invented to serve that project: to coordinate contribution, to reward those who added value, to allocate consumption in proportion to productive input. This is why a market without humans — an AI-to-AI economy, a system of machines optimizing for machines — is not a more efficient market. It is not a market at all. It is resource allocation pointed at nothing, optimizing toward an objective set by whoever wrote the last configuration. The market's purpose is inseparable from human wanting. Strip out the wanting and the purpose dissolves.

Third: systems require tension to self-correct. This is not an economic observation. It is a structural one, visible everywhere from thermodynamics to evolutionary biology to political philosophy. A system with no internal tension has no mechanism for detecting error, no gradient to climb, no signal distinguishing better from worse. Markets have tension because humans have competing preferences. Democracies have tension because humans have competing values. Both are self-correcting precisely because they are never fully resolved. The goal of either system is not to eliminate tension. The goal is to channel it productively. A system that eliminates tension — even in the name of efficiency, even without malice — eliminates its own capacity to self-correct.

These three foundations are not novel. Economists from Hayek to Acemoglu have touched different facets of them. What is less examined is what follows when all three are placed together and run forward against the specific trajectory of artificial intelligence — not as a future speculation, but as a logical consequence of where the present trend line points. That is the argument this essay makes. It does not require a villain. It does not require a catastrophe. It requires only that we take the trend line seriously and follow it to where it ends.

The Market Is a Feedback Loop Built on Human Tension

Market is not a natural phenomenon. It is a human construction — a formalization of a very old social instinct: reward those who contribute to the collective, and let that reward determine how much they can consume in return. The market did not invent value. It abstracted and scaled something humans were already doing around every fire and in every village. Productive capacity generates income. Income generates consumption. Consumption signals what to produce next. The loop is self-sustaining because the engine at its center — human dissatisfaction, human desire for improvement — never runs out of fuel.

This is not merely descriptive. It is structural. The disproportionality of the loop — consumption symmetrically dependent on value generated — is not a flaw to be corrected. It is the source of the market's dynamism. Unequal consumption capacity creates differentiated demand. Differentiated demand creates competition. Competition creates the texture of an economy: luxury markets, niche markets, aspirational markets, the entire layered complexity of commercial life. Remove the disproportionality and you flatten the texture. The loop still turns but it no longer generates heat.

Marx called a version of this the overproduction crisis — the tendency of capitalist systems to produce more than their workers, stripped of wages, can consume. He was right about the mechanism and wrong about the timeline, because industrialization ultimately created new categories of work and reconstituted the loop. The question this article asks is whether that reconstitution is possible when the displacement is cognitive rather than physical. That question has a different answer.

A system that eliminates tension eliminates its own capacity to self-correct.

This is true of markets. And it is equally true of societies and democracies. The mechanism is identical all the way up.

The First Domino: When Development Becomes Frictionless

The vertical most vulnerable to AI displacement right now is not customer service or logistics or even legal research, though all of those are exposed. It is software development — the production of the tools that produce everything else. This is not coincidental. Developer automation is the recursively powerful displacement: automate the builders and you accelerate the automation of everything downstream.

The process is already underway. Tools like Claude Code represent early democratization — the reduction of the friction between having an idea and producing a working system. But democratization is not yet collapse. There is still an adoption inertia. Non-developers still face barriers — terminal interfaces, technical vocabularies, the cognitive overhead of working in unfamiliar environments. These barriers are eroding. When a sufficiently accessible version of this capability reaches mass adoption — when the barrier to producing a functional application becomes equivalent to the barrier to writing an email — the democratization will have reached its tipping point.

At that point, producing value becomes frictionless. Anyone can build. Everyone builds. And the first consequence is not collapse but a production explosion — a fog of war period in which the market floods with new capability and it is genuinely unclear who will capture the value being created. But all speculations lead to two broad paths:

Path One — Consolidation Large players adapt using infrastructure advantage. They become the vendors of consolidated AGI — the mother platforms through which all individual production flows. They win the production war. The fog clears and they own everything.
Path Two — Open Source Large players fail to adapt fast enough. Open source fills the vacuum. A community-built ant-colony AGI emerges organically — many agents, many contributors, one emergent capability. We have already seen the prototype: MCPs, skills platforms, agent ecosystems.

The Compass Problem: What Does Better Even Mean?

The standard economic argument against this conclusion is historical. Agricultural revolution, industrialization, the computing revolution — each appeared catastrophic at the inflection point and each ultimately reconstituted the labor loop, though painfully and over generations. New categories of value emerged. New jobs appeared. The system adapted.

This argument has real force. The World Economic Forum projects 92 million jobs displaced by 2030, offset by 170 million new ones created — a net gain, on paper, of 78 million jobs. Goldman Sachs estimates only 2.5% of US employment faces displacement risk, with disruption transitory, typically resolving within two years as new categories emerge. These are not fringe views. They are the considered positions of serious institutions.

A correction in framing, not in fact Both projections are snapshots on an exponential curve. They measure current AI capability against current labor markets. What changed between 2020 and 2022 was significant. What changed between 2022 and 2024 was more significant still. Projecting from a point on an exponential curve and concluding the slope is gentle is a known methodological failure — it is like measuring the internet's economic impact in 1997 and concluding it is marginal. The WEF and Goldman arguments are not wrong about their moment. They are wrong about which moment they are describing.

But even granting the historical argument its full force, it rests on an assumption that is weaker this time than it has ever been. Previous automation displaced physical capability. Humans adapted by applying cognitive capability to new problems. AI displaces cognitive capability. There is no known third category of distinctively human productive contribution to retreat to. The historical analog breaks precisely at the point where it needs to hold.

There is also a physical brake on this trajectory worth acknowledging. Citadel Securities argues the doomsday thesis suffers from a recursive technology fallacy — that energy and compute constraints naturally limit infinite AI expansion. The data center buildout required to run frontier AI at civilizational scale is genuinely staggering: the water consumption, the electricity draw, the chip supply chains. These are real constraints. They do not invalidate the directional argument. They slow it. The question has always been one of when, not whether.

The LLM ceiling — a separate but related brake Current large language models are trained recursively on text from the internet. That corpus is finite and increasingly self-referential — models training on outputs of models. Researchers broadly expect a capability plateau within the next one to two years on this architecture. The next leap likely requires something structurally different: multimodal training, embodied learning, or the very ant-colony multi-agent architecture described above. Each transition buys time. None of them changes the destination.

The deeper problem, though, is not the labor market at all. It is what economists do not yet have adequate language for: the compass problem. Markets do not just allocate resources. They aggregate information about human preference, human dissatisfaction, human desire for improvement. The price signal is not just a coordination mechanism — it is a direction signal. It tells the system what "better" means at any given moment. That signal is generated by the unsatisfied human observer. Remove the unsatisfied human observer from the production side of the loop and you do not merely lose workers. You lose the instrument that tells the system where to point.

A corporation that has captured the market — or an open AGI that has automated all production — is an optimization machine without a defined objective. It will optimize. But optimize toward what? The answer, in the absence of the human signal, can only be: toward what it has been told to optimize toward by whoever configured it last. Which means the compass problem is not just an economic problem. It is a power problem. Whoever defines the objective function of the final system holds a form of power that has no historical precedent. And the frightening part? This happens when every player — corporation, developer, open source contributor, policymaker — makes a locally rational decision.

What emerges from the aggregate of rational choices is a ghost economy: GDP still registers, corporate profits still climb, the numbers still look like growth. But the value being created never circulates back through the human consumer economy. It accumulates at the infrastructure layer, above the loop, outside the loop. The loop continues to spin. It just no longer connects to anything. Economists have begun calling this ghost GDP — growth that registers in the numbers and nowhere else.

No villain is required. The globally catastrophic outcome is the aggregate of rational choices.

When the Fog of War Settles

When the first domino tips, and production explodes, the compass problem tells us the signal eventually breaks. What it does not tell us is who is left standing when it does. That depends on which path the fog of war resolves into — Consolidation, or Open Source — and the unsettling answer is that it does not matter.

Both paths arrive at the same destination. The difference is in who presides over the endpoint. In neither case does the market recover its dynamism, because in neither case does the fundamental condition for that dynamism — humans contributing differentially and being rewarded differentially — survive.

Path One — The Corporate Capture

The corporation wins the fog of war. It owns production. It owns distribution. In a world where producing value requires no human labor, the corporation has no structural need to employ humans. Consumption capacity — which was always downstream of productive contribution — begins to drain out of the human population. The corporation finds itself the vendor of everything to a market that can afford progressively less. It has won completely and inherited nothing.

Path Two — The Open Source Emergence

Large players fail to adapt. Small, flexible actors fill the space. The internet offered a historical preview: individual contribution, open protocols, community maintenance, emergent standards. The same pattern is already visible in the MCP ecosystem and agent platforms. A distributed ant-colony AGI emerges — not one model but many coordinating agents, each contributing a skill, together constituting a general capability. At some point, one app connects them all. The open AGI arrives. And then, again: development has automated itself. There is nothing left to build. Everyone consumes the same open system. The loop goes flat.

The UBI Trap and the Collapse of Democratic Plurality

The standard policy response to labor displacement is Universal Basic Income — a floor of consumption capacity distributed equally, decoupled from productive contribution. As a humanitarian measure it is defensible. As an economic solution it fails on first principles.

The market's dynamism depends on disproportionality. UBI, by design, provides uniform consumption capacity. Uniform consumption produces uniform demand. Uniform demand eliminates competitive differentiation. The market does not recover — it simplifies toward stagnation. The loop still turns but generates no improvement signal.

A discriminated UBI — points-based, behaviorally weighted, tied to contribution of some kind — appears to solve this by restoring disproportionality. But it immediately raises a question that is more disturbing than the economic one: who defines the point system? On what values? By what ideology? In a market as uniform as the one described here, with production and distribution consolidated in either a corporate or an open-AGI architecture, there will be one source of infrastructure, one source of capability, one source of economic power. Discriminated UBI administered by that source is not an economic policy. It is a political theology. One ideology, one methodology, one definition of what behavior deserves reward.

Democracy emerged after hundreds of wars and revolutions, precisely because human civilization concluded that no single ideology should hold that kind of definitive power. Plurality of thought, competition of ideas, distributed power — these were not decorative features of the democratic experiment. They were the structural mechanism by which societies retained the capacity to self-correct. They were, in the most precise sense, the political equivalent of market tension.

A sufficiently uniform society — one source of production, one source of distribution, one system administering access — cannot sustain genuine plurality. The feedback loop that democracy depends on breaks by the same mechanism as the market feedback loop. Not through conquest. Not through tyranny. Through the elimination of the structural tension that made self-correction possible. I leave you with one question.

If uniformity of power is the terminal state of this trajectory — if every path through the AI transition leads to one source of capability, one definition of value, one ideology administering access — is there any structural mechanism that could reintroduce tension artificially? And if such a mechanism were designed and implemented, would what remained still be recognizable as democracy?
